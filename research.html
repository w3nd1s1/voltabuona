Research Question

The emergence of Linked Open Data (LOD) and Semantic Web technologies has profoundly reshaped the ways in which cultural heritage information is structured, published, and accessed. Through the use of formal ontologies and knowledge graphs, cultural heritage institutions are now able to represent complex datasets in a machine-readable and interoperable format, enabling large-scale integration, querying, and reuse of cultural information across different platforms and domains.

Despite these advances, current cultural heritage knowledge graphs remain primarily oriented toward cataloguing and documentation purposes. Their main function is to ensure the accuracy, consistency, and interoperability of descriptive metadata rather than to provide exhaustive interpretative or contextual information about cultural assets. As a result, although such systems are highly effective in representing formal properties—such as identification data, classification, and administrative information—they often provide limited support for narrative depth, historical contextualisation, and user-oriented interpretation.

This structural limitation leads to a persistent gap between two complementary dimensions of cultural heritage representation. On the one hand, there is the formal semantic layer, which ensures data integrity, standardisation, and machine-readability. On the other hand, there is the interpretative and contextual layer, which would allow users to engage more deeply with the cultural, historical, and artistic significance of an asset. In many cases, this second layer remains incomplete or implicit, particularly with regard to detailed historical reconstructions, architectural interpretations, multimedia documentation, and explicit semantic connections to external knowledge sources.

Importantly, this limitation does not reflect a deficiency in the underlying ontologies or data models. Rather, it arises from the inherent scope of cataloguing systems, which are designed to prioritise authoritative description and semantic consistency over narrative expansion. While this approach is essential for ensuring interoperability and long-term preservation, it can also restrict the accessibility and interpretability of cultural heritage data for non-specialist users, researchers in interdisciplinary fields, and the general public.

Within this context, Artificial Intelligence—particularly in the form of Large Language Models (LLMs)—introduces new possibilities for addressing this imbalance. By leveraging their capacity to process, synthesise, and generate structured knowledge from large textual corpora, such models may support the enrichment of existing cultural heritage representations by identifying missing contextual information and proposing coherent descriptive expansions that complement, rather than replace, authoritative metadata.

The central research question guiding this study is therefore the following:

How can AI-assisted semantic enrichment enhance the completeness, contextual depth, and interpretative accessibility of cultural heritage knowledge graphs, while preserving their semantic consistency and full compatibility with the ArCo ontology and Linked Open Data principles?

This research question deliberately focuses on the tension between two essential requirements in the field of digital cultural heritage: the need to increase the expressive and interpretative richness of knowledge representations, and the necessity of maintaining strict adherence to established semantic standards that guarantee interoperability, reliability, and machine-readability. The aim is not to modify or replace existing cataloguing frameworks, but to explore how additional layers of AI-generated semantic content may contribute to a more comprehensive and usable representation of cultural heritage within existing knowledge graph infrastructures.
